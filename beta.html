Why is it important?

You Could Pay More for Less Internet

The European Commission and Council want to adopt a regulation that would enable internet providers to charge extra for any specific online service. Instead of the open internet that has been such a boost for the economy and free speech worldwide, we would see a "first class" internet for companies that can afford it, leaving the rest of us with fewer destinations and a "second class" service.

Internet Providers could act as Gatekeepers

Unless the regulation is fixed, internet providers will also be able to block and filter content without any judicial oversight, meaning they can decide what you can and can't do online. Internet service providers should not behave like the police of the internet. They should not be allowed to define which content you can and cannot access.

Freedom of Speech and Innovation is in Danger

Right now big companies are on the same level as our Blogs or Podcasts. Unless the regulation gets fixed, these companies will be in the fast lane on the data highway, leaving start-ups and non-commercial websites in the slow lane.

On 4th April 2014 the European Parliament voted for strong net neutrality rules. This victory was yours because many of you contacted Members of the European Parliament (MEPs) to take a stand for a free and open internet.

The EU member states waited a long time until they adopted an incoherent, misleading text on the 2nd of March that would destroy the net neutrality we all fought for so hard. This text would create paid fast-lanes, internet blocking and allow price discrimination in the EU.

Now we enter a phase called "trialogues", where all three EU institutions negotiate on the final text. And now is the time to contact your MEPs and ask them to stand by their decision as of April 2014 and don't let big telecom companies destroy the internet and our freedom online.

Net neutrality is the founding principle of the internet. It guarantees that all data packets are treated equally and that everyone has access to the whole internet. This principle guarantees that the internet is as diverse, innovative and free as we all know it. Telecom companies want to change that. They want to establish new business models based on discrimination and restrictions. We cannot gamble away the economic and social public value of internet, just to "help" a handful of companies.

We need your help to prompt your representatives to protect the open and neutral internet - ask them to protect network neutrality!

TIMELINE TO SAVE THE INTERNET

  • 30 June 2010: European Commission launches consultation on net neutrality
  • 11 September 2013: European Commission proposal for the “Telecon Single Market” includes a fake version of net neutrality, which actually went against it.
  • 14 January 2014: First savetheinternet campaign launches to convince the Parliament of the importance of net neutrality.
  • 3 April 2014: European Parliament votes to protect net neutrality. This is the decision we have to protect!
  • 1 October 2014: NGOs and Industry calling for European governments to support strong net neutrality rules in the Council.
  • 26 February 2015: The FCC approves rules that ban blocking, throttling and paid prioritization online. Net neutrality is officially protected in the US.
  • 27 February 2015: The European Council’s draft [LINK: http://data.consilium.europa.eu/doc/document/ST-6405-2015-INIT/en/pdf] is discussed at Working Group level. As a result of extensive pressure from the large Member States it re-inderts loopholes that would undermine net neutrality.
  • 3 March 2015: The European Council agrees on a final text. As a result of extensive pressure from large Telecom companies it re-introduces loopholes that would undermine net neutrality and allow for all possible forms of discrimination.[Link1 https://edri.org/eu-council-proposals-protecting-open-internet-episode-1-phantom-neutrality/ and LINK2 https://edri.org/open_internet_clownwars/]
  • 11 March 2015: The final battle begins. Negotiation starts between European Commission, Parliament and Council.
  • 11 March 2015: IMCO shadows meet
  • 18 March 2015: ITRE Shadows meet
  • 30 March 2015: 1st trialogue

How to Contact Your MEP

Act now to tell your representatives to protect our rights and freedoms. The best way to do this is by calling a Member of the European Parliament (MEP). But you can also send them a Fax, a letter or an E-Mail - we provide you with all the information, and calling them is free of charge.

General advice

Stay polite and be yourself. Whatever happens, don't forget the basic rules of courtesy and common sense. Whether you agree or disagree with the individual answering to you, and whatever the views of other members of her/his political group, don't give a negative image of people who are advocating with the same purpose as you.

Most of the time, you will talk with a Parliamentary assistant, and not directly with a MEP. It's not a problem: engage in the conversation. Assistants play an important role in the development of the MEPs' positions.

If a question to which you don't have the answer comes up, don't panic. You are not expected to be an expert, only a concerned citizen. Tell the MEP you will research the answer and contact him/her back with more information, and come and ask us.

If you're still not comfortable with the arguments, don't give up. Ask what is the MEP's position on the subject, and ask what their arguments are.

During a phone call don't hesitate to offer to call back with more information, to meet the MEP, to send documents, references, etc. Sometimes, Parliamentary assistants will ask you to send an e-mail. Don't hesitate to call back later to check if they've read it and what they thought of it.

Fax

With our Fax Tool you can send your message to an Member of the European Parliament (MEP). It will be printed out in the office of your representative. There will be a phyical token of your opinion and the chances of it being read are much higher. But even more so it is important that you personalize your message. Everything that we write about E-Mails, also applies to Fax.

Mail

You can also contact your MEP by email. Their addresses are available in the mail widget above.

Some people sometimes propose to send generic form emails to all MEPs (and even to those who do not vote on the related subject). We believe that such emails are counterproductive. MEPs and their assistants know how to use a spam filter as well as you, and those emails end up in spam folders quickly. Generic form emails give the impression that you do not want to take the time to get interested in the matter, and do not reflect the number of people involved in it (a single person can send several messages). Worse, such emails increase the risk that MEPs do not read the personalized emails on the same dossier, and finally hurting your cause.

The best solution is to send personalized emails based on your own approach and your knowledge of the matter (Remember: you are not expected to be an expert, only a concerned citizen) and, if possible, according to the positions of the MEP's political group.

Tweet your MEP

Tweeting to a MEP is a very effective way of getting them involved. Twitter discussions are public which helps generating attention, simply because MEPs don't want to lose a debate in public. 140 characters for one Tweet are easily written and don't take much of your time.

Facebook

If you make the effort to write a text it is also helpful to send this text to the MEP via Facebook. You can either send them a private message or even write on their Facebook page (if their privacy settings allow you to do so). To post publicly about net neutrality gives the issue more traction and can spark a public discussion in our favour.
